# What Really Happens When You Open a Website

In the previous article, we met the four main characters that power the Internet.

- 🖥️ **Client**
- 🏢 **Server**
- 🌍 **Internet Service Provider (ISP)**
- 🛣️ **Routers**

Each one plays an important role whenever you browse the web.

But we stopped just before the interesting part.

We never actually watched them work together.

So let's do exactly that.

Imagine you're sitting in front of your laptop.

You open your browser...

Type:

```text
google.com
```

And press **Enter**.

The page appears almost instantly.

To us...

It feels effortless.

But behind those few milliseconds lies an incredible chain of events involving computers, routers, data centers, and thousands of kilometers of fiber-optic cables.

Let's follow that journey together.

## 🚀 Mission

Throughout this article, we'll follow **one single request** from the moment you press **Enter** until Google's homepage appears on your screen.

## Step 1 — Your Browser Has a Small Problem

When you type:

```text
google.com
```

Your browser immediately encounters its first obstacle.

It knows the **name**.

But it has absolutely no idea where Google actually lives.

Humans prefer names because they're easy to remember.

Imagine trying to remember this every time you wanted to search something:

```text
142.250.183.78
```

Not very practical.

That's why we use names like:

- google.com
- youtube.com
- github.com
- openai.com

They're designed for humans.

Unfortunately...

Networks don't understand names.

They understand **IP addresses**.

Every computer connected to the Internet has one.

Before your browser can send even a single request...

It first needs to discover Google's IP address.

So it asks someone for directions.

---

## Meet DNS — The Internet's Phonebook

Think about your phone for a moment.

When you want to call your friend...

You don't remember their phone number.

You simply search for:

**Priya**

Your phone quietly finds the number for you.

DNS works almost exactly the same way.

Instead of storing people's names and phone numbers...

It stores **domain names** and their corresponding **IP addresses**.

Whenever you type:

```text
google.com
```

Your computer sends a request to a **DNS Server**.

The question is surprisingly simple.

> **"Do you know the IP address for google.com?"**

The DNS server quickly searches its records...

Finds the correct IP address...

And sends it back.

Only now does your browser know where Google's servers actually are.

<strong>CORE CONCEPT</strong>

Humans navigate the Internet using **domain names**.

Computers navigate the Internet using **IP addresses**.

**DNS (Domain Name System)** acts as the translator between the two.

Without DNS, you'd have to memorize numerical IP addresses for every website you wanted to visit.

</div>

## A Tiny Shortcut You Never Notice

Here's something even more interesting.

Your browser doesn't always contact a DNS server.

If you've recently visited the same website...

Your computer may already remember its IP address.

This temporary memory is called a **DNS Cache**.

Instead of asking the DNS server again...

Your browser simply reuses the stored address.

Think of it like calling your best friend.

The first few times, you search for their number.

Eventually...

You remember it by heart.

Your computer does exactly the same thing.

Caching helps websites load even faster by avoiding unnecessary DNS lookups.

## Step 2 — The Journey Finally Begins

Now your browser knows Google's address.

The destination has been found.

It's finally time to send the request.

But here's another question.

How does your laptop actually reach a computer that might be thousands of kilometers away?

It can't magically send data directly to Google's servers.

Instead...

Your request begins a long journey across the Internet.

It leaves your browser...

Travels to your home Wi-Fi router...

Gets forwarded to your **Internet Service Provider (ISP)**...

Then starts hopping across multiple routers around the world.

Each router performs one simple job.

It looks at the destination IP address...

Finds the best available path...

And forwards your request to the next router.

Eventually...

After passing through many different networks...

Your request reaches Google's data center.

What's fascinating is that your request doesn't travel along one giant cable directly to Google.

Instead...

It moves through many independent networks.

Each router only worries about one thing:

> **"Where should I send this next?"**

No single router knows the entire journey.

Each one simply forwards the request to the next best destination until it finally reaches Google's servers.

## 💡 Did You Know?

When you load a modern website, your browser usually doesn't make **just one request**.

It requests:

- 📄 The HTML page
- 🎨 CSS stylesheets
- ⚙️ JavaScript files
- 🖼️ Images
- 🔤 Fonts
- 🎥 Videos
- 📦 API responses

Each of these can follow a similar journey across the Internet.

That's why browsers constantly send and receive dozens—or even hundreds—of requests in the background.

## Step 3 — Why Websites Don't Travel as One Giant File

At this point...

Your request has finally reached Google's servers.

The server understands exactly what you're asking for.

Now it has to send the webpage back.

But here's something that surprises many people.

The server doesn't simply bundle everything into one enormous file and send it across the Internet.

Instead...

It does something much smarter.

It **breaks the webpage into thousands of tiny pieces** before sending anything.

These pieces are called **packets**.

---

## Why Split Everything Into Packets?

Imagine you're moving to a new house.

You own a large bookshelf.

Would you carry the entire bookshelf on your back?

Probably not.

You'd take it apart...

Pack the pieces into smaller boxes...

Move each box separately...

Then reassemble everything once you arrive.

That's exactly how the Internet works.

Instead of sending one giant block of data...

It sends lots of smaller packets.

Each packet contains only a tiny portion of the original information.

Whether it's:

- 📄 An HTML document
- 🎨 A CSS file
- ⚙️ JavaScript
- 🖼️ Images
- 🎥 Videos

Everything eventually becomes packets.

<strong>CORE CONCEPT</strong>

The Internet never thinks in terms of **webpages** or **videos**.

It only knows how to move **packets**.

Everything you use online—whether it's Netflix, Instagram, GitHub, or YouTube—is ultimately transmitted as thousands (or even millions) of packets.

</div>

## What's Inside a Packet?

Think of each packet as a tiny parcel being shipped through the postal system.

Every parcel needs more than just its contents.

It also needs a shipping label.

A network packet is no different.

Every packet contains two important parts.

### 📦 Payload

This is the actual data.

It could be:

- Part of an image
- Some HTML
- A few lines of CSS
- A JavaScript file
- Even a tiny fragment of a YouTube video

### 🏷️ Header

The header acts like the address label on a package.

It contains information such as:

- 📍 Source IP Address
- 🎯 Destination IP Address
- 🔢 Packet Number
- ⚙️ Protocol Information
- ✅ Error Checking Data

Without this information...

Routers wouldn't know where to send the packet.

And your computer wouldn't know how to put everything back together again.

---

## Every Packet Travels Its Own Journey

Here's where things become really fascinating.

You might imagine every packet following exactly the same path.

That's not always true.

One packet might travel through Chennai.

Another might travel through Mumbai.

Another could even pass through Singapore before reaching its destination.

The Internet doesn't force every packet to stay together.

Instead...

Routers make independent decisions for each one.

Their only goal is simple.

> **Find the fastest available path.**

That means two neighbouring packets can take completely different routes across the world...

...and still arrive safely.

---

<div className="brutal-border bg-brand-purple text-white p-6 shadow-brutal-sm my-10">

## 💡 Did You Know?

The Internet doesn't reserve a dedicated road for your data.

Instead, packets from **millions of people** travel together across the same cables.

At any given moment...

The same fiber-optic cable may be carrying:

- Someone streaming Netflix
- A student attending an online class
- A gamer playing Valorant
- A company making a bank transaction
- You reading this very article

All of these packets travel side by side without getting mixed up because every packet carries its own destination address.

</div>

## Step 4 — Putting Everything Back Together

Eventually...

The packets arrive at your computer.

But they don't always arrive in the same order they were sent.

For example...

The server might send them like this:

```text
Packet 1
Packet 2
Packet 3
Packet 4
Packet 5
```

But your computer could receive them like this:

```text
Packet 3
Packet 1
Packet 5
Packet 2
Packet 4
```

At first glance...

That sounds like chaos.

Fortunately...

Every packet carries its own sequence number.

Your browser patiently waits until enough packets have arrived.

Then it rearranges them into the correct order.

Only after that does it begin rendering the webpage.

This entire process usually happens so quickly that you never notice it.

To you...

The page simply appears.

## The Server's Reply Completes the Journey

Once Google's server has finished preparing the webpage...

It sends thousands of packets back across the Internet.

Those packets travel through multiple routers...

Cross several different networks...

Eventually reach your ISP...

Pass through your home router...

And finally arrive at your browser.

## Step 5 — The Internet's Invisible Highways

By now...

Your browser has successfully received thousands of packets from Google's servers.

But there's one question we haven't answered yet.

**How did those packets travel across the world so quickly?**

Many people imagine that Internet traffic is constantly bouncing off satellites floating in space.

It sounds reasonable.

After all...

Satellites help with GPS, television broadcasts, and weather forecasting.

Surely the Internet works the same way...

Right?

Surprisingly...

**Not for most of the Internet.**

In reality, more than **95% of international Internet traffic** travels through **undersea fiber-optic cables** stretched across the ocean floor.

These cables are the true backbone of the modern Internet.

---

## Beneath the Ocean Lies the Internet

Imagine connecting every continent with thousands of kilometers of incredibly thin glass cables.

Now imagine shining pulses of light through those cables at nearly the speed of light.

That's exactly how fiber optics work.

Instead of sending electrical signals like traditional copper wires...

Fiber-optic cables transmit data using **light**.

Every packet you've learned about travels as tiny flashes of light through these fibers.

These cables stretch across oceans, connecting countries and continents together.

Whether you're:

- 🌏 Browsing a website hosted in the United States
- 🎥 Watching a YouTube video from Japan
- 💻 Pushing code to GitHub
- 🤖 Using ChatGPT

There's a very good chance your data is travelling through one or more of these underwater cables.

## Why Not Use Satellites?

If satellites already exist...

Why don't we simply send all Internet traffic through them?

The answer comes down to one word:

**Latency.**

A signal sent to a satellite has to travel thousands of kilometers into space...

Then back down to Earth.

That extra distance introduces noticeable delays.

Fiber-optic cables, on the other hand...

Provide:

- ⚡ Lower latency
- 🚀 Faster transmission speeds
- 📦 Higher bandwidth
- 💰 Lower operating costs

That's why companies like Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and many telecom providers invest billions of dollars building and maintaining undersea cable networks.

Satellites are still useful...

Especially in remote locations...

But for the majority of global Internet traffic...

Fiber optics are the preferred choice.

## What Happens If a Cable Breaks?

At this point, you might be wondering...

"If so much of the Internet depends on these cables...

What happens if one of them gets damaged?"

It's a good question.

And surprisingly...

Cable failures happen more often than you might think.

Ships dragging anchors...

Fishing equipment...

Earthquakes...

Even underwater landslides...

Can damage these cables.

Would that disconnect the Internet?

Thankfully...

No.

## The Internet Was Built to Handle Failure

One of the Internet's greatest strengths is that it was never designed around a single path.

Instead...

It behaves more like a giant road network.

Imagine you're driving to another city.

Halfway through your journey...

You discover that the highway has been closed.

Do you give up and go home?

Of course not.

You simply follow a different road.

The Internet works exactly the same way.

Every router continuously exchanges information with neighboring routers.

If one connection suddenly becomes unavailable...

Routers quickly calculate another available path.

Your packets are automatically redirected.

Most of the time...

You won't even notice it happening.

## 💡 Did You Know?

Every second...

Millions of routing decisions are made across the Internet.

Routers are constantly checking:

- Which path is the fastest?
- Which path is congested?
- Which path is unavailable?
- Which route should packets take next?

This dynamic decision-making allows the Internet to continue operating even when parts of the network fail.

## Putting Everything Together

Let's revisit everything we've learned.

Imagine opening your browser one more time.

You type:

```text
google.com
```

Here's what happens behind the scenes.

1. 🌐 Your browser asks a DNS server for Google's IP address.

2. 📍 DNS returns the correct IP address.

3. 📤 Your browser creates an HTTP request.

4. 🏠 The request leaves your device and reaches your home router.

5. 🌍 Your ISP forwards the request into the global Internet.

6. 🛣️ Multiple routers forward the request toward Google's data center.

7. 🌊 If necessary, the request crosses oceans using undersea fiber-optic cables.

8. 🏢 Google's servers receive the request.

9. 📦 The webpage is split into thousands of packets.

10. 🚀 Those packets travel back across the Internet.

11. 🧩 Your browser reassembles every packet into the original webpage.

12. 🎉 Google's homepage appears on your screen.

All of this usually happens in well under **one second**.
